Visualizing Global Development Through Demographic and Health Indicators

A compact visual study of how fertility, migration, infant mortality, aging, and service-sector employment complicate income-based definitions of development.

A Project Overview

Abstract

Traditional definitions of developed countries often rely heavily on economic measures such as Gross National Income (GNI) per capita. However, income alone may not fully capture the broader social, demographic, and health conditions associated with development. This project explores whether social indicators such as fertility, infant mortality, migration, and demographic inequality provide additional insight into how development should be understood and classified.

Using data from the World Bank World Development Indicators and country group classifications from the United Nations World Economic Situation and Prospects framework, we examine global patterns across fertility rates, infant mortality, migrant share, and sex-based mortality differences. Through a combination of choropleth maps, box plots, and interactive dashboards built using Python, Plotly Dash, and web-based visualization tools, the project visualizes how countries transition through different stages of development over time.

The findings suggest that development is multidimensional and uneven rather than purely income-based. While many countries experience substantial improvements in healthcare and demographic outcomes, regional disparities and demographic inequalities often persist despite broader economic growth. Overall, the project demonstrates that social and demographic indicators can provide a more comprehensive understanding of development than income classifications alone.

B Before the Visualizations

Project Motivation

Development is often summarized through income, but countries with similar income levels can still differ greatly in health, demographic change, and migration patterns. Looking at social indicators alongside economic measures helps reveal those differences.

This project compares several indicators to ask a broader question: what becomes visible when development is treated as a multidimensional process rather than a single ranking? The visualizations below connect income-based classifications with fertility, migration, and infant mortality outcomes across countries and over time.

Key Terms

These definitions provide a quick reference for the measures and classifications used throughout the visualizations.

GNI per capita

Gross National Income (GNI) is the total income earned by the residents of an economy during an accounting period. GNI per capita divides that income by the total population, making comparisons across countries easier.

GNI = GDP + income earned from abroad - income payable abroad

GNI per capita = GNI / total population

When this indicator is expressed in constant prices, it has been adjusted for price changes over time.

Development groups

The project compares developed economies, economies in transition, and developing economies using United Nations classifications. These categories provide a useful framework, but they do not capture every social difference within each group.

Fertility rate

The average number of children a woman would have if current age-specific birth rates remained the same throughout her reproductive years.

Migrant share

The share of a country's population made up of international migrants. It can reflect labor demand, mobility, conflict, geography, and regional integration.

Infant mortality rate

The number of infants who die before reaching one year of age per 1,000 live births in a given year.

1 Demographics

Fertility and Development

Fertility decline is one of the most widely observed demographic patterns associated with development. Lower fertility often emerges alongside urbanization, women's education, rising living costs, and expanded access to healthcare and family planning.

Developed economies generally cluster at lower fertility levels, while many developing economies remain higher. This pattern supports the broader demographic-transition argument, but it is not perfectly linear: some middle-income countries have already experienced sharp fertility declines, while some wealthier economies still show distinct national patterns shaped by cost of living, family policy, urbanization, and social expectations.

2 Migration

Migrant Share by Development Group

This figure compares international migrant stock as a share of total population across developed economies, economies in transition, and developing economies. The current static image uses a boxplot to summarize the distribution; a future scatter-violin version could strengthen the section by showing both the overall distribution and the individual country-level observations. Migration can reflect labor-market attraction, economic opportunity, political stability, historical ties, and regional integration.

Developed economies generally show the highest migrant shares and the greatest variation, which suggests that migration is not only related to income but also to labor demand, education opportunities, and openness to international mobility. Economies in transition tend to fall in the middle, while many developing economies remain lower. However, the outliers are especially important because they show that high migrant share alone does not automatically mean broad-based development; it may also reflect temporary labor systems, displacement, or regional migration corridors.

3 Geography

Geographic Distribution of Outliers

This map examines the geographic distribution of outlier countries within the developing economies group in 2020. Rather than treating these outliers as random exceptions, the map connects unusually high international migrant stock to specific structural drivers, institutional labor systems, conflict-driven displacement, and globalized urban economies.

Using the 1.5×IQR rule, sixteen outlier countries were identified within the developing economies group, of which nine exceed the 30% threshold and are labeled on the map: Qatar, United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, Bahrain, Singapore, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Hong Kong SAR, and Oman. These countries do not share the same migration story, which is why the map is organized into two zoomed insets, the Gulf Region and East/Southeast Asia to facilitate country-specific interpretation rather than one general explanation.

The map shows that extreme migrant-share outliers are geographically concentrated in the Gulf states and high-density city-economies in East and Southeast Asia, suggesting that migration intensity is shaped by specific structural conditions rather than development status alone.

Read full explanation

The map reveals a striking geographic concentration: nearly all extreme outliers above 30 percent cluster in two distinct subregions, the Gulf states and high-density city-economies in East and Southeast Asia, suggesting that migration intensity is shaped by specific structural conditions rather than development status alone.

The Gulf cluster, including Qatar at 77.8 percent, the UAE at 76.0 percent, Kuwait at 68.9 percent, Bahrain at 53.0 percent, Saudi Arabia at 42.2 percent, and Oman at 39.1 percent, shares a common driver. Since the oil boom of the 1970s, GCC economies have structurally relied on imported labor to sustain their industries because their indigenous populations are small relative to their economic scale. The kafala sponsorship system, which legally ties workers to their employer, has institutionalized this dependence. Labor is predominantly drawn from South and Southeast Asia through decades of established bilateral agreements.

Jordan, at 45.5 percent, reflects a different mechanism: successive waves of forced displacement, from Palestinians in 1948, Iraqis in 2003, and Syrians in 2011. This means refugee intake, rather than economic recruitment, is its primary driver.

Singapore at 46.1 percent and Hong Kong SAR at 39.6 percent represent a third mechanism: small, globalized urban economies with persistent demand across the full skill spectrum, from financial professionals to domestic workers recruited from the Philippines and Indonesia.

This geographic clustering highlights that even within the developing economies category, migration is driven by fundamentally different mechanisms: institutionalized Gulf labor systems, conflict-driven displacement, and globalized urban labor markets. These mechanisms are difficult to capture through aggregate statistics alone.

4 Health Transition

Interactive Infant Mortality Visualizations

These dashboards expand the analysis by examining how infant mortality changes across countries, sex categories, and time. They allow users to explore long-term health transition, compare regional development patterns, and observe how sex-based mortality inequality behaves as countries modernize. The interactive format is useful because it lets readers move beyond a single summary statistic and compare specific countries, regions, and years.

Infant Mortality and Health Transition Explorer

This dashboard combines a global infant mortality map with a health transition scatter plot. Users can filter by country, region, year, and sex category to examine how infant mortality rates decline over time. The map highlights geographic differences in health outcomes, while the scatter plot helps show whether countries with similar income or development classifications experience similar levels of child survival. Together, the views make it easier to identify both broad global progress and countries that remain outside the general trend.

The website shows readable static previews for accessibility and for users who cannot load the full dashboard immediately. The full dashboard adds the most analytical value because it allows country search, filtering, year animation, and sex-category comparison. Readers should use the controls to compare whether infant mortality decline happens evenly across regions or whether some countries improve faster than others despite similar development classifications.

Male-Female Infant Mortality Gap

This visualization isolates the male-female infant mortality gap across countries and over time. Positive values indicate higher male infant mortality, while negative values indicate higher female infant mortality. Tracking the gap separately is important because overall infant mortality can decline while sex-based differences remain visible, shrink at different speeds, or occasionally move in unexpected directions.

This chart is fully usable on the website. The animated years, tooltips, zoom, and pan features allow readers to focus on specific countries and compare how the male-female gap changes as infant mortality declines. Open the standalone Plotly file for a larger view when presenting or when reading labels in detail.

Health transition

Most countries experienced substantial declines in infant mortality between 1960 and 2024, showing a broad global health transition connected to improved healthcare access, vaccination, sanitation, nutrition, and maternal-child health systems.

Uneven progress

The pace of improvement differs significantly across regions and development stages. Some countries move quickly toward lower infant mortality, while others improve more slowly because of conflict, inequality, limited infrastructure, or uneven access to healthcare.

Persistent gaps

Sex-based disparities can remain visible even as healthcare systems modernize. This suggests that development should be evaluated not only by whether mortality declines overall, but also by whether improvements are shared evenly across groups.

5 Synthesis

Outro

This project demonstrates that development is multidimensional and cannot be fully understood through income-based classifications alone. By comparing fertility, infant mortality, migrant share, and other demographic indicators across countries and development groups, we observe that social outcomes often reveal important differences that traditional economic measures may overlook.

Together, the visualizations suggest that development should be viewed as a broader social process shaped by healthcare access, demographic transition, migration patterns, regional history, and long-term structural change. The strongest next step would be to continue expanding the interpretation beneath each figure so that readers understand not only what the visualizations show, but why those patterns matter.
